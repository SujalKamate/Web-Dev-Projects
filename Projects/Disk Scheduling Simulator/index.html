What is Disk Scheduling?

Operating systems use **Disk Scheduling** to allocate the order in which mechanical disk read/write requests are processed. Since mechanical drives require a physical **disk arm** to sweep across concentric **cylinder tracks**, disk seek travel accounts for the largest overhead delay in secondary storage access.

  • Seek Time: The time required for the disk arm to move the read/write head to the desired cylinder track.
  • Rotational Latency: The time spent waiting for the platter sector to rotate underneath the head.
  • Total Seek Distance: The sum of cylinder gaps crossed during the schedule. Scheduling algorithms aim to minimize this number to maximize disk throughput.

SCAN & LOOK (Elevator Mappings)

Named after passenger elevators, these algorithms change directions only after serving requests:

  • SCAN (Elevator): The disk arm travels in one direction, serving tracks along the way, all the way to the **physical edge of the disk** (0 or MaxCylinder). Then it reverses direction and sweeps back.
  • LOOK: An optimization of SCAN. The head only travels as far as the *last request* in its current direction. If no requests are pending further up, it immediately reverses direction without reaching the physical edge.

Circular Algorithms (C-SCAN & C-LOOK)

Circular variants offer more uniform wait times by serving requests only during one direction of the sweep:

  • C-SCAN: Sweeps in one direction, serving requests to the boundary. Upon hitting the edge, it performs a **rapid return wrap-around** to the opposite edge *without serving requests on the return sweep*, then continues.
  • C-LOOK: Similar to C-SCAN, but the wrap-around jump occurs immediately after serving the last request in that direction, wrapping back to the first request on the opposite side.
